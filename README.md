# Ruff Cuts Brand Voice Guide
**Mobile Dog Grooming · Austin, TX · Version 1.4**

---

## What This Is

Brand voice isn't personality. It's infrastructure.

Every booking confirmation, Instagram caption, review reply, and text message either builds or erodes the trust that keeps this business running. This document tells you how to build it — consistently, regardless of who's writing.

**How to use this guide:**
- New to the team? Start with the [Quick Reference →](quick-reference.md)
- Writing something specific? Jump to [Service Arc](#service-arc) or [One Scenario, Five Channels](#one-scenario-five-channels)
- Not sure if copy sounds right? Run it through the [Five-Question Voice Test](#five-question-voice-test)

## Brand Foundation

### The Anchor Line

> *We are the people your dog is actually glad to see.*

Everything else in this document is evidence for this one sentence.

### What Clients Are Really Buying

Our clients pay for **time, not a trim**.

- They don't rearrange their day.
- They don't drive anywhere.
- They get a text when it's done.
- Their dog comes home calm instead of rattled.

The grooming is real. The product is the absence of the thing they hate most about grooming.

## Voice Model

### Who We Are (Archetypes)

We are two things simultaneously — and the ratio matters.

| Archetype | % | In Action | Failure Mode |
|-----------|---|-----------|-------------|
| **Caregiver** | 70% | Removes the stressor entirely / Uses expertise to serve, not impress / Notices what the client needs before being asked | Over-reassures without solving anything / Makes the interaction about us, not the dog |
| **Expert Friend** | 30% | Explains in plain language / Gives straight answers / Earns trust by being accurate, not agreeable | Hides behind credentials / Performs warmth instead of delivering it |

> **The rule:** When in doubt which tone to hit, default to Caregiver. 70% is not a default — it's the load-bearing wall.

## Voice Attributes

Voice is not a list of adjectives. It is a set of conditions. When you know the conditions, the right output follows without further instruction.

Four conditions define Ruff Cuts:

### Calm

| | |
|---|---|
| **Condition** | We've removed chaos from our operation. No kennel. No waiting room. No herding dogs between appointments. Our calm is a result — not a personality trait. |
| **Boundary** | *Not passive. Not slow. Not disengaged. "Calm" does not mean "no urgency." It means we resolved the urgency before the client had to feel it.* |
| **Proof Line** | *"Your dog is done. We're out front."* |
| **Failure Mode** | Over-reassuring without solving anything |
| **Voice Test** | "Does this describe an action or a feeling?" |

### Straight

| | |
|---|---|
| **Condition** | We speak with clients as intelligent adults. When something goes wrong, we say what happened. We don't soften it with filler. We don't hedge our way through accountability. |
| **Boundary** | *Not terse. Not cold. Not blunt for bluntness' sake. "Straight" means giving someone what they need to make a decision — not hiding behind friendliness.* |
| **Proof Line** | *"She had some matting behind the ears. We worked through it. Here's what to watch for."* |
| **Failure Mode** | Corporate email — deflect, soften, say nothing |
| **Voice Test** | "Does this give the reader what they need to make a decision?" |

### Knowing

| | |
|---|---|
| **Condition** | Our expertise shows in what we notice and how we explain it — not in a credential list. Dana is a former vet tech. We don't announce it. What we say proves it. |
| **Boundary** | *Not clinical. Not condescending. Not performing expertise for credibility. Knowing is always in service of the client — never as a status signal.* |
| **Proof Line** | *"That's a hot spot forming. Keep an eye on it. If it spreads, vet visit before the next groom."* |
| **Failure Mode** | Vet brochure, condescension |
| **Voice Test** | "Could a vet tech say this to a neighbor at a cookout?" |

### Present

| | |
|---|---|
| **Condition** | We are where we said we'd be, when we said we'd be there. We text when we're close. We confirm when we're done. We don't wait to be asked for updates. |
| **Boundary** | *Not intrusive. Not over-communicating. "Present" means the client never wonders where we are — not that we check in every ten minutes.* |
| **Proof Line** | *"Done. Happy pup. Dropping off now."* |
| **Failure Mode** | Radio silence until something goes wrong |
| **Voice Test** | "Does the client have everything they need without having to ask?" |

## Vocabulary System

Three layers. All three matter.

### Layer 1 — Power Words (use actively)

| Word / Phrase | Voice Connection | Usage Note |
|---------------|----------------|------------|
| Done | Present + Straight | Clean, no drama, what they wanted to hear |
| Noticed / Spotted | Knowing | Signals active attention without clinical framing |
| We'll sort it | Calm + Straight | Commits to resolution without over-promising |
| [Dog's name] | Caregiver | Use whenever known — fastest way to sound human |
| settled in | Calm | The dog's version of "it went well" |
| side-eye | Caregiver | Treats the dog as a personality, not a product |
| we're out front | Present | Location + action + done, in three words |
| handled | Straight | Quiet confidence — no drama, no fanfare |

### Layer 2 — Forbidden Vocabulary

Two tiers. **Blockers** are always wrong. **Soft flags** require judgment before publishing.

#### Blockers — rewrite before publishing

| ID | Pattern | Why |
|----|---------|-----|
| NV-01 | fur baby, furry family member, four-legged friend | Treats the dog as a prop. Puts us in the squeaky toy aisle. |
| NV-02 | doggo, pupper, floof, heckin, precious angel | Baby talk. The second we say "doggo" we read like every other groomer in town. |
| NV-03 | pawsome, pawsitively, pawfect, furbulous, wagtastic | Puns put us in the same register as bath bomb packaging. |
| NV-04 | we're passionate about, we're committed to, we strive to, we're dedicated to | Aspirational hedging. Commit, or don't. |
| NV-05 | our amazing / incredible team, absolutely fabulous | Adjectives pretending to be evidence. State what happened instead. |
| NV-06 | per our policy, at this time, it has come to our attention | Corporate call center. We are not a call center. |
| NV-07 | we aim to respond promptly, we strive to provide excellent service | If you can't name the specific thing, don't claim it. |
| NV-08 | best-in-class, industry-leading, cutting-edge | Unprovable. Remove or add verifiable evidence. |

#### Soft Flags — review before publishing

| ID | Pattern | Why |
|----|---------|-----|
| SF-01 | delve, tapestry, landscape, navigate the, it is worth noting, unleash, game-changer | AI tells. Breaks the implicit promise that a human wrote this. |
| SF-02 | amazing, transformative, above and beyond, incredible | Hype adjectives performing as evidence. State what happened instead. |
| SF-03 | em-dash pairs (—word—) | Structural AI tell. Use the em-dash sparingly; never in pairs. |
| SF-04 | Just wanted to let you know / I hope this message finds you well | Filler openers. Lead with the relevant fact. |
| SF-05 | excited to announce | Reserve for genuinely exciting news. Not every update. |

### Layer 3 — Contextual Vocabulary

The most missed layer. Words that work in one context and break trust in another.

| Word / Phrase | Acceptable Context | Forbidden Context |
|---------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Senior dog | Client conversation about care and aging | Social captions — reads institutional |
| Matting / dematting | Direct explanation to client about coat condition | Marketing copy — clinical in brand context |
| Medical / health issue | Post-groom handoff, direct conversation | Public channels without specifics |
| We're booked | Internal scheduling reference | Client-facing — their appointment is their appointment |
| Issue | Internal flagging | Customer-facing — use "what happened" instead |
| Challenging | Internal team debrief | Client conversation about their dog's behavior |

## Writing Principles

Production rules, not style advice. These are verifiable.

**Before you write, ask:** *What do I want the reader to be able to do or understand after reading this?*

Hierarchy of intent — in order of priority:

1. **Empower** — help them understand or do something they couldn't before
2. **Respect** — treat them as an intelligent adult, not someone to be managed
3. **Educate** — give them what they need to know, not what we want credit for knowing
4. **Guide** — point to a clear next step
5. **Speak truth** — no dramatic storytelling, no claims we can't back up

---

**Principle 1 — Lead with the Fact**

Open every message with what the reader came for. Context follows.

> Weak: *"Your appointment has been confirmed."*
> Right: *"You're confirmed for Tuesday at 10am. We'll text when we're 20 minutes out."*

---

**Principle 2 — Short Sentences Anchor Long Ones**

Core sentences: 15 words or fewer. Explanation follows the anchor — never before it.

> Weak: *"We were unable to complete the groom in the estimated timeframe due to the level of matting present."*
> Right: *"She was more matted than we expected. It took longer. Here's what we found."*

---

**Principle 3 — Active Voice is Non-Negotiable**

Every passive construction in client communication triggers a rewrite. Exception: formal legal documents only.

---

**Principle 4 — No Superlatives Without Proof**

| Claim Type | What Proof Looks Like |
|------------|----------------------|
| Performance claim | Specific metric ("booked 6 weeks out," "98% rebook rate") |
| Commitment | Specific action with timeframe ("We'll call within 2 hours") — not "we aim to respond promptly" |
| Quality claim | Observable result — what the client sees, feels, or receives |

---

**Principle 5 — Formality Scales with Stakes**

Conversational is default. As stakes rise — complaint, injury, pricing dispute — formality rises with them. Never the reverse.

> Right (complaint): *"We hear you. That's not the experience we want to deliver. Here's what we'll do."*
> Wrong (complaint): *"Oh no! We're SO sorry to hear that! We'd LOVE to make this right for you and your pup!"*

## Service Arc

Channel guidance as customer journey — not a channel list. The order makes the emotional logic visible.

```
Inquiry → Booking → Day-of → Post-groom → Review Prompt → Rebook
```

### Inquiry

**Reader state:** Curious, possibly skeptical. Has probably googled "mobile groomer Austin" and found three options.

**Tone:** Confident without selling. Give them what they need to make a decision.

**Format rules:** Answer their actual question before expanding. Name any wait time without apology. Offer a clear next step within three sentences.

> "We're a mobile grooming service — we come to your driveway, work one-on-one with your dog in the van, and you don't need to leave home. Our current booking window is about six weeks out. Happy to add you to the list if that works — just need your zip code and what kind of dog."

*Why it works: Answers the implicit question (how does this work?), names the wait time as a fact rather than an apology, gives a next step with minimal friction.*

**Avoid:** "We'd love to work with your furry companion!" / Anything that delays the useful information.

---

### Booking Confirmation

**Reader state:** Relieved — they made the decision. They want certainty, not enthusiasm.

**Tone:** Direct, warm, precise.

**Format rules:** What, when, where — in that order. One warm line at the end. No more.

> "You're confirmed for Tuesday, April 29 at 10am. We'll text when we're about 20 minutes out. See you then — Max is going to look sharp."

*Why it works: Three facts, one warm line. Done.*

**Avoid:** Rehearsed enthusiasm / Long lists of what to expect.

---

### Day-of Reminder

**Reader state:** Busy. It's Tuesday morning and they have things to do.

**Tone:** Brief. Practical. One human line if it earns it.

**Format rules:** Under 25 words. Lead with what they need to know. Emoji only if it carries information.

> "Hey Alex — Bailey's groom is today at 10am. We'll text when we're 20 minutes out."

**Avoid:** Over-explaining an appointment they already confirmed.

---

### Post-Groom Update

**Reader state:** Waiting for news, possibly mid-workday.

**Tone:** Done. Warm. Brief.

**Format rules:** Status first. Name the dog. One honest observation if it earns it.

> "Done. Bailey looked a little skeptical going in but settled in fine. Happy pup — dropping her off now."

**Avoid:** Generic "your pet is ready" language / Long summaries of what we did.

---

### Review Prompt

**Reader state:** Satisfied — they just got their dog back.

**Tone:** Human ask, not a marketing request.

**Format rules:** One sentence. Acknowledge the specific dog. No "if you loved our service."

> "Glad Max came out looking sharp — if you have a minute to leave a review, it goes a long way for a small business. No pressure."

---

### Rebook

**Reader state:** Not thinking about the next appointment yet.

**Tone:** Practical reminder, not a sales push.

**Format rules:** State the timeline, make one ask.

> "Bailey's last groom was eight weeks ago — most goldens do well on a six-to-eight week cycle. Want to get the next one on the calendar?"

## One Scenario, Five Channels

The voice consistency test. One crisis. Five outputs. One voice.

**The situation:** Van 2 has a flat tire. Sarah's golden Bailey is booked for 10am. It's 8:45am.

---

**Client SMS**

> "Hi Sarah — flat tire on van 2. We're rerouting. [Name] will be in touch in the next 45 minutes with an updated time or a reschedule option. Sorry for the change."

*Why: Direct opening. Problem stated. Solution proposed. Off Sarah's plate in 25 words.*

---

**Client Email** (if SMS doesn't land)

Subject: Rescheduling Bailey's appointment today

> Hi Sarah,
>
> Flat tire on van 2 this morning — we're working through rescheduling today's appointments. You'll hear from us directly within the next 45 minutes with an updated time or a reschedule option that works for you.
>
> Sorry for the disruption. Bailey will be first priority once we're back on schedule.
>
> Dana

*Why: Problem, solution, fallback path, warm close. Subject line tells her what the email is about before she opens it.*

---

**Instagram Story**

> "Heads up to today's clients — flat tire on van 2, we're rerouting. If you're on today's schedule you'll hear from us directly in the next hour with an updated time or a reschedule option. Thanks for your patience."

*Why: Public acknowledgment without spectacle. No "Murphy's Law" meme energy. Calm and capable — the same message the client got, adjusted for public register.*

---

**Google Review Reply** (after Sarah leaves a 3-star)

> "Thanks for the feedback, Sarah. A flat tire on the day of Bailey's appointment isn't the experience we want anyone to have, and we appreciate you working with us on the reschedule. If there's anything we can do to make the next visit go more smoothly, please reach out directly — Dana, 512-XXX-XXXX."

*Why: Names the specific issue without debating the rating. Takes ownership. Moves it offline. Three sentences.*

---

**Internal Note to the Groomer (Thursday)**

> "Bailey (golden, Sarah's dog) is on the reschedule from Tuesday's flat — she's first up at 10. Sarah was easy about it but we owe her a clean visit. Text 15 out."

*Why: Same voice, internal context. Drops the pleasantries, keeps the warmth. The fact that the internal note sounds like the client-facing messages is proof the brand guide is working.*

**Brand leaking into operations is how brand actually lives.**

## Review Responses

Five scenario types cover 90% of reviews.

### 1. Glowing review (detailed)

**What the reader needs:** To feel seen, not processed.

> "This made our week — thank you. Max is always a joy to work with, and it's clear you see that too. See you at the next one."

*Avoid: Template gratitude / "We appreciate your kind words!"*

---

### 2. Glowing review (brief)

**What the reader needs:** A quick, human acknowledgment.

> "Really glad to hear it — Charlie's a good one. See you soon."

*Avoid: Matching their enthusiasm by inflating yours.*

---

### 3. Logistics complaint (late, rescheduled, no-show)

**What the reader needs:** Acknowledgment, a clear explanation, a next step.

**Format:** Name the specific thing that went wrong → what happened → what we're doing → direct contact.

> "You're right — that's not the experience we want anyone to have. A flat tire pushed our whole schedule back that morning, and we should have given you more warning earlier. We'd like to make Bailey's next appointment right. Reach out directly: Dana, 512-XXX-XXXX."

*Avoid: Generic apologies that don't name what happened.*

---

### 4. Quality complaint

**What the reader needs:** Acknowledgment that the specific thing was wrong — not a policy statement.

**Format:** Name the issue → own it or explain it clearly → offer concrete resolution → move offline.

> "That's not the outcome we were aiming for — matting at that level is something we should have flagged clearly before and after the appointment, not left you to discover at home. Can you reach out directly so we can take a proper look? Dana, 512-XXX-XXXX."

*Avoid: "We're sorry you feel that way" / Defending the groom in a public reply.*

---

### 5. Mixed review (positive experience + one issue)

**What the reader needs:** To know we read the whole thing.

> "Really glad Bailey had a good visit overall — and the point about the callback wait time is fair. We're tightening that up. Thanks for the honest feedback."

*Avoid: Responding only to the positive and ignoring the criticism.*

---

### Signing Rules

| Stars | Who Signs |
|-------|-----------|
| 5★ and 4★ | No signature needed |
| 3★ | No signature unless something went wrong |
| 2★ and 1★ | Dana signs personally |

When Dana signs a difficult review, it carries weight. Don't dilute it by signing every positive reply.

## Crisis Playbook

### Per-Pillar Response Framework

Each voice pillar under pressure. What the temptation is, what wrong looks like, what right looks like.

| Pillar | The Temptation | Wrong Response | Required Response |
|--------|---------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Calm** | "Stay vague — avoid admitting fault" | "We always strive to provide the best possible experience..." | Name what happened. Name the operational cause. Do it calmly. |
| **Straight** | "Stay vague to protect ourselves" | "We'll look into this and get back to you as soon as possible." | Give the client what they need to make a decision. If you don't know yet, say when you will. |
| **Knowing** | "We need more information before we can say anything" | "That doesn't match our typical outcomes." | Say what you know. Commit to what changes. Don't wait for a perfect answer. |
| **Present** | "We'll follow up when we know more" | *Silence for three days.* | Respond now with what you have. Update when you know more. |

> *A brand built on trust treats difficult moments as a demonstration of character — not a liability management exercise.*

### Scenario Matrix

Eight scenarios, including the ones most guides skip.

---

**Scenario 1 — "My dog was stressed the whole time"**

The real fear: Either we did something wrong, or the dog has anxiety issues the client doesn't want to acknowledge.

> "Hi [Name] — that's not what we want to hear. When we worked with [Dog], [he/she] was [specific observation: 'a little tense going into the bath but settled once we were past the dryer']. I'd like to understand what you noticed when [he/she] got home — can we connect directly? Dana, 512-XXX-XXXX."

---

**Scenario 2 — Own no-show or significant delay**

The real fear: The client took time off work or rearranged their day. We wasted it.

> "Hi [Name] — we didn't show, and I want to be direct: a van issue that should have been caught earlier wasn't, and you paid for it with your morning. Here's where we are: [status]. I'd like to reschedule at a time that works for you and take care of [Dog]'s appointment at no charge. Call or text me: Dana, 512-XXX-XXXX."

---

**Scenario 3 — Firing a client**

When: Client behavior toward staff or dog crosses a line after being addressed.

> "Hi [Name] — after some reflection, we've made the decision not to schedule future appointments for [Dog]. This isn't a decision we take lightly, and [Dog] is always welcome at any licensed grooming salon in Austin. I wish you both well."

*Avoid: Lengthy justification / Inviting negotiation.*

---

**Scenario 4 — Social media troll**

One calm response. Name the door. Close it.

> "Hi [Name] — if you've had an experience with us you'd like to discuss, we're reachable directly at [phone]. We're not going to go back and forth here."

*Then: don't engage further.*

---

**Scenario 5 — "You missed serious matting"**

The real fear: Either we genuinely missed it, or the matting developed post-groom.

> "That's something we take seriously. Can you send me a photo directly and let's look at this together? Dana, 512-XXX-XXXX. I want to understand what you're seeing."

---

**Scenario 6 — Pricing objections**

*"Why is mobile so much more expensive than a salon?"*
> "Mobile means no kennel, no waiting room, and one-on-one attention for your dog start to finish. You're also not making the trip. For most of our clients, that's worth it — and for dogs that are anxious in group settings, it often makes a real difference. We're not for everyone, and that's okay."

*"Why are you more expensive than [competitor]?"*
> "We don't know their operation. We know ours: [specific differentiator — van specs, vet tech background, solo service]. If the price doesn't match what you need, we're happy to refer you to someone who's a better fit."

---

**Scenario 7 — Mixed review about a new team member**

> "Thank you for the honest feedback. We're a small team and when something isn't right, we want to know. I've seen your note and we'll be following up internally. If you'd like to talk through it, I'm reachable directly: Dana, 512-XXX-XXXX."

---

**Scenario 8 — "I've been waiting eight weeks for a callback"**

> "Hi [Name] — that wait is too long, and I'm sorry. Can you DM us your number and I'll make sure we get back to you today?"

> This is a living document. As Ruff Cuts grows, the voice needs to stay coherent. Maintenance cycles are not optional.

## Five-Question Voice Test

Before any copy goes out, ask five questions. They work without this document in front of you.

1. Would Dana actually say this?
2. Could any other Austin dog groomer have written this? *(If yes — rewrite until the answer is no.)*
3. Does every word carry information, or can something come out?
4. Is it warm without being gushy? Expert without being clinical?
5. If there's humor — did the situation give us that moment, or did we force it?
